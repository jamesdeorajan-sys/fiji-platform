Why the Yasawa Islands Are Unique

The Yasawa Islands are a chain of 20 volcanic islands stretching 90 kilometres northward from the Mamanuca Islands off Fiji's west coast. Until 1987, they were closed to tourists — which has left them with a raw, unspoiled quality you won't find closer to Nadi.

There are no casinos, no golf courses, no nightclubs. What you'll find instead is Fiji as it was — traditional villages, pristine reef, kayaking and snorkelling from the beach, and an unhurried pace that makes even busy people slow down.

Bula tip: The Yasawas are not a resort destination in the conventional sense. Internet connectivity is limited or non-existent at many properties. This is a feature, not a bug — but worth knowing before you book.

How to Get to the Yasawa Islands

There are two ways to reach the Yasawas from Nadi: by ferry (affordable, social) or by seaplane (fast, scenic). The great majority of visitors take the Yasawa Flyer from Port Denarau Marina.

Step 1 — Get to Port Denarau Marina

Port Denarau Marina is 20–30 minutes from Nadi Airport. You need to be at the marina before your ferry departure. Pre-booking a transfer from the airport is the easiest approach.

Step 2 — The Yasawa Flyer Ferry

The Yasawa Flyer is a large high-speed catamaran operated by South Sea Cruises. It departs Port Denarau daily at 8:30am and makes stops throughout the Mamanuca and Yasawa chains.

Ferry Pass Options

  • Bula Pass (2 days): A single stop and return. Best for short trips to the closer Yasawas.
  • Bula Pass (multiple days): Hop-on hop-off for 5, 7, or 10 days. Maximum flexibility for island-hopping.
  • Single leg ticket: Available for fixed itineraries. Cheaper but less flexible.

Seaplane to the Yasawas

Turtle Airways operates seaplane transfers to the northern Yasawas. The flight takes 30–40 minutes versus 5+ hours on the ferry — significantly more expensive but dramatic. Resorts like Yasawa Island Resort include seaplane transfer in their rates.

Nanuya Lailai — The Blue Lagoon

The most photographed spot in the Yasawas. The Blue Lagoon bay has electric-blue, gin-clear water in a sheltered bay with white sand beaches. Film location of the 1980 movie starring Brooke Shields.

Accommodation ranges from budget bures to mid-range beach lodges. Day-trippers can visit via the Yasawa Flyer with a few hours on the island before the ferry returns.

Budget Guide to the Yasawas

The Yasawas are one of the few places in Fiji where genuine budget travel is possible. A network of family-owned bure operations offers accommodation with meals included at a fraction of resort prices.

  • Budget bure (meals included): FJD $80–160 per night per person
  • Mid-range lodge: FJD $250–500 per room per night
  • Boutique luxury: FJD $500–900 per room per night
  • Ultra luxury (Yasawa Island Resort): USD $2,000+ per night all-inclusive
Budget tip: All meals at budget bures are included in the daily rate. This is traditional Fijian hospitality — your hosts cook fresh food and eat together. It is one of the most authentic Fiji experiences available at any budget.

Things to Do in the Yasawa Islands

Snorkelling

The Yasawa reefs are among Fiji's healthiest — limited tourism pressure has preserved excellent coral coverage. Most bures and resorts loan snorkel gear free of charge. The outer reefs around Naviti, Nacula, and Matacawalevu are exceptional.

Village Visits

Traditional Fijian villages throughout the Yasawas welcome respectful visitors. The proper protocol is to bring sevusevu — a gift of kava root (yaqona) — which your accommodation host can arrange. Village visits typically include kava ceremony, traditional song and dance, and craft demonstrations.

Kayaking

The calm lagoon waters between islands are perfect for kayaking. Most accommodations provide kayaks. The paddle between some of the closer island stops is achievable for fit paddlers.

Sawa-i-Lau Caves

Near Nacula Island, these sacred limestone caves have been used for thousands of years. Visitors swim through a submerged passage into a cathedral-like cavern filled with natural light. One of Fiji's most extraordinary natural experiences.

Manta Ray Snorkelling (May–October)

Naviti Island's Manta Ray Bay sees regular manta ray encounters from May to October. Snorkelling with mantas in the wild (not a feeding attraction) is an experience that defines Yasawa travel for many visitors.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do you get to the Yasawa Islands from Nadi?
Take the Yasawa Flyer ferry from Port Denarau Marina (20–30 min transfer from Nadi Airport). The ferry departs daily at 8:30am and stops at islands throughout the chain. Travel times range from 1 hour 45 minutes (southern Yasawas) to 5.5 hours (northern tip).
What is the Blue Lagoon and how do I visit it?
The Blue Lagoon is a bay on Nanuya Lailai Island, famous for its extraordinary colour and as the setting of the 1980 film. It's a ferry stop on the Yasawa Flyer — you can stay overnight at one of several bures and lodges on the island, or visit as a day-tripper.
Are the Yasawa Islands suitable for families?
Yes, but they suit families differently than Denarau resort hotels. The Yasawas are best for families with older children (10+) who are comfortable with simpler accommodation and unplugged from devices. Young children may be better served by the family resort infrastructure at Denarau or the Coral Coast first.
Is there WiFi in the Yasawa Islands?
Limited to non-existent at most budget and mid-range accommodation. Some upscale resorts have satellite WiFi. Consider this a feature of the destination — the Yasawas are one of Fiji's best places to completely disconnect.
When is manta ray season in the Yasawas?
Manta rays are most commonly encountered around Naviti Island from May to October (dry season). Peak months are June–August. Sightings are not guaranteed but are frequent during these months.
